⟨ SCENE 02 — THE MATHEMATICS ⟩
Good. You've got the intuition. Now the real thing.

In practice we don't use paint. We use modular exponentiation — raising a number to a power and taking the remainder after dividing by a large prime number p.

The operation is: g^x mod p.
Computing this is fast. Running it in reverse — finding x when you only know the result — is called the Discrete Logarithm Problem, and for large primes it is computationally impossible.
So g and p are public — anyone can know them. But the exponent x stays secret, and the result I send is useless without knowing x?
Precisely. The protocol works like this:

You pick a secret a. Your contact picks a secret b. You exchange g^a mod p and g^b mod p publicly. Then each of you raises the other's value to your own secret exponent. The maths guarantees you land on the same number K — your shared key. The enemy, watching every exchange, cannot compute K without solving the discrete log.

But then, how do I know I chose good values for my parameters ?
That's a good question, for p you only need to choose a prime number that is big enough. For g it is a bit more complicated.
A good g must be a primitive root mod p — meaning its order equals p-1, so it cycles through all possible values before repeating. This maximises the search space Eve faces.
For p = 23, the primitive roots are: 5, 7, 10, 11, 14, 15, 17, 19, 20, 21 — exactly 10 of them.
On the other side, non-primitive roots are dangerous. g = 2 mod 23 has order 11, meaning it only ever produces 11 distinct public values instead of 22. Eve's search space is instantly halved — a serious weakness.

Finally for your personal secret key a or b, you just need it to be a big positive random number.
⟨ SCENE 04 — THE REAL WORLD ⟩
With 21 candidates you found it in a handful of tries. Satisfying, wasn't it?

Now consider this. In production systems, the prime p is not 23. It has 2048 bits — that's roughly 617 decimal digits. The number of candidates Eve would need to search is approximately 10^617. Even with every GPU on Earth running in parallel, that search would take longer than the age of the universe.
But computing g^a mod p on a 2048-bit prime — doesn't that take forever too?
Not at all. That's the elegant asymmetry at the heart of this protocol.

The forward computation — g^a mod p — uses a technique called fast modular exponentiation. On modern hardware, it runs in milliseconds.

The reverse — finding a from the result — has no efficient algorithm. The best known approach, the Number Field Sieve, still requires around ~10^90 operations at this key size. We call this modular exponentiation a trap door function. I again prepared a program just for you. Run it yourself and see the difference.
